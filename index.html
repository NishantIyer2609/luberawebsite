The Problem: Digitization Has Stalled

Primary Care Clinics Remain Largely Undigitized

Only ~20% of primary care and small ambulatory clinics use EMR systems in studied settings.

Scale of Unmet Demand

India has 100,000+ independent clinics delivering the majority of ambulatory care.

Corporate hospitals and larger chains are largely digitized with EMRs and clinical systems. Small clinics predominantly rely on paper registers and manual recordkeeping.

This is not merely a technology availability gap—it reflects fundamental constraints in cost, infrastructure, workflows, and support.

Why Digitization Fails in Small Clinics

For most small clinics, the economics simply do not work: they are asked to invest ₹20,000+ in hardware and pay ₹1,000–₹5,000 per month for software, despite seeing only 15–25 patients a day. System setup typically demands IT support, configuration, and training, and any bugs or failures directly disrupt clinic hours. As a result, paper-based workflows remain cheaper, more predictable, and lower risk for these practices.

Why Now? The ABDM Mandate

India's Healthcare System Is Entering the 2026 ABDM Wave

The Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission (ABDM) is a ₹1,600+ crore national program to create a unified digital health ecosystem used by every doctor and patient.

Core Requirements:

Every citizen receives a unique ABHA ID—like an Aadhaar for health
Medical records are expected to be digitally created, shared, and linked to ABHA ID
Clinics, hospitals, labs, and pharmacies integrate into ABDM network via EMR providers

The Regulatory Pressure: With ABDM–PMJAY mandates, digitization is no longer optional. Empanelled clinics under PMJAY now have limited time to procure hardware, adopt compliant software, and transition away from paper.

Recent High Court Rulings and Growing Patient Expectations

High Courts vouched for clinics to adopt digital prescription standards.

Growing Patient Expectations for doctors to access records via ABHA ID instantly.

How low can hardware cost and power consumption go while delivering full EMR, e-prescription, ABDM, and practice management—with equal or better performance, reliability, and UI/UX than urban clinics?

2. Network Reality

How little bandwidth can the system survive on while still syncing with ABDM infrastructure, cloud records, and workflows?

Ground Truth:

  • • Many clinics operate at ~100 kbps
  • • Most ABDM-compatible EMRs stall or fail at these speeds

3. Zero Learning Curve & Zero Failure

Can a doctor start using the system without training, face no configuration or installers, encounter no bugs, and have minimal error paths and malware exposure?

Current State:

No specialized clinical desktop achieves this today.

Lubera's Architecture: What Makes This Possible

Lubera is built on a novel frame-buffer Linux architecture using first-in-industry system design practices.

This architecture allows full-fledged, NHA-compliant medical and tooling software to run on ultra-low-resource hardware—previously considered impossible.

Lubera is not cheaper software. It is a new class of clinical infrastructure built for India.

The Doctor Experience

Instant Patient Onboarding via ABDM

Patient arrives. Patient scans the clinic's ABHA QR code (printed card or phone) and gets redirected to ABDM App. After consent to share records, Lubera instantly pulls complete medical history from the national grid—past diagnoses, medications, allergies, lab results, prescriptions from other cities.

The fragmented file system becomes a unified timeline.

The 3-Click Prescription

Interface obsessively optimized for speed. Common diagnoses and medications intelligently mapped by specialty.

Standard prescription workflow: Select diagnosis → Pick medications → Generate.

Total time: under 30 seconds. The system watches quietly in the background, flagging drug interactions and dosage risks before prescription finalizes. One click sends digitally signed e-prescription to patient's WhatsApp or prints wirelessly.
